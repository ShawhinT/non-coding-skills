# Thesis Examples

Opinion posts, trend analysis, and bigger ideas. Notice the narrative arc — these don't just state a thesis, they tell the story of why it matters. Personal evidence is compressed into proof, not expanded into content.

## Example: AI Expanding Human Potential

Here is something I believe (that people will disagree with).

>> AI will expand human potential, not diminish it.

As the tasks that computers can do become more sophisticated, there's a natural fear that they will replace much of the work we (i.e. humans) do, making us obsolete.

However, I don't see that happening.

Today:
- Claude Code writes ~99.9% of the code I generate
- ChatGPT advises me on almost all content, strategy, and product ideas
- My most time-consuming tasks are being automated with Agent Skills

Yet, I'm busier than ever.

Why?

Because AI removes constraints.

Five years ago, building a software product felt like a pipe dream.

Today, I'm building product #3.

But this isn't unique to AI. It's the pattern technology has followed for centuries.

When tools remove constraints, humans don't stop working. We aim higher. We pursue harder problems, explore stranger ideas, and build things that were previously unimaginable.

Nature has no bottom. She will always present new problems to solve and new secrets to reveal.

AI just gives us more leverage to go after them.

## Example: Where Will Humans Go

AI automation is here… So where will humans go?

Over the past few weeks, I've used Claude to automate gathering leads, sales outreach, making email intros, drafting keynote decks, and editing YouTube videos.

This didn't require any fine-tuning or complex AI engineering.

It was all done using MCP, agent skills, and having Claude build itself command line tools.

(And this just feels like the beginning.)

In the face of this rapid automation, a natural question is:

→ If AI can automate most things, what will humans do?

The answer reminds me of a coffee chat I had with David Evans a few months back, who said that the work humans do will shift into the (infinitely-long) tail.

Let me explain.

Work, and its associated value, follows the 80-20 rule, i.e., 80% of the value is generated by 20% of the work (the precise quantities are likely different; the rule is a qualitative one).

This is because work can be described by a Pareto distribution (see the image below), where most work is low-value (on the left-hand side of the plot) and a minority of work is high-value (on the right-hand side).

The rise of agentic AI is expanding the work computers can do, eating up much of the low-value work humans do now.

However, here's the key point.

Even if this AI frontier keeps expanding over the next few years, the tail (i.e. the right part of the plot) is practically infinitely long.

So even if 99% of work gets automated, there is still endless high-value work that humans can (and should) do.

I'm already seeing this personally in my work. Taking tedious tasks like video editing and sales outreach off my plate frees me up to do higher-leverage work like refining my target ICP and the marketing strategies I use to reach them.

Is this a trend you see in your work too?

## Example: AI Fears

AI is scary.

Employees fear losing their jobs.

CEOs/boards fear falling behind (and losing their companies).

While these fears tend to clash, they have the same root cause.

→ Ignorance

And when humans face the unknown, fear is our default.

The solution isn't reassurance.

It's education.

Because once you understand AI clearly, you can see:
- What's possible today vs what's hype
- Where AI amplifies people vs replaces tasks
- What's likely to change in your industry over the next 6–12 months

How are you approaching AI education right now?

## Example: 2 Ways AI Transformations Fail

I recently asked an AI Transformation lead: "Why do transformations fail?"

Here's what they said…

Way 1: Lack of Adoption
Employees are hesitant about AI.
The implicit thought is, "If I become more productive, what does that mean for headcount?"
This leads to some quietly resisting adoption and slowing down progress.

Way 2: Lack of Support
Even if there is bottom-up support, another blocker is a lack of top-down support.
AI might be important to leadership, but just not important right now.
This leads to stalled progress due to a lack of resources and urgency.

How are you seeing AI transformations fail?
