# Channel: Sales Emails (warm)

Use this framework when writing a 1:1 sales email to someone who already knows Shaw — a past client following up on their engagement, a warm lead after a discovery call, a referral whose context is clear, or someone who booked-then-ghosted.

This is **not** the framework for cold outreach to strangers (→ `channel-outreach.md`) or general email drafts without a conversion intent (→ `email-writer`).

## Before you draft

Lock these:

- **Who is this person and what's the context?** Past client? Booked-then-ghosted prospect? Post-call warm lead?
- **What's the single next step?** Reply with a date? Send the intake form? Pay the invoice? Confirm the proposal?
- **Is there recent history you can reference?** A specific thing said on the call, a recent LinkedIn post of theirs, a project they mentioned. Use it.
- **Customer-language source.** If it's a past client, their own quotes are the best material.

## Core principles (warm-email-specific)

### 1. Short wins

Dave Gerhardt's 1-line sales email works because most sales emails suffer from bloat. When you have the warm context, you don't need to re-establish anything.

- If the email could be three lines, don't write five.
- The goal is to get a reply, not to pre-close the deal. A reply gives you something to work with.
- Long setup paragraphs signal uncertainty. Short direct emails signal confidence.

### 2. Ask a question

Questions create reply-pressure in a way statements don't. The whole email can be one question plus signature.

- Example (Dave Gerhardt style): *"Hey [name] — quick one. Did you see our team is opening 5 spots next month for exec coaching? Worth a conversation?"*
- The question should make "no" easy and "yes" specific.

### 3. Reference the specific context

The warm email's superpower is that you can reference things cold emails can't:

- "You mentioned on our call that X was eating your week..."
- "Following up on that question about Y..."
- "Saw your post about Z — thought of you because..."

If you have the context, use it. If you don't, ask why you're sending a "warm" email without it.

### 4. Advice beats pitch

Same as cold outreach, even with warm leads. Ask for input, offer a next step that's useful to *them* regardless of whether they buy.

- "I've been thinking about the situation you described — here's what I'd try first. Want me to walk through it in 15 minutes?"
- That framing makes the next step valuable *to the reader* whether or not they end up buying.

### 5. Personalize the opening line (it becomes preview text)

Gmail/Apple Mail show the first line as preview. A generic "Hi [name], I wanted to reach out about..." burns the preview. A specific opener gets opens.

- **Burns preview:** "Hi Shaw, I wanted to reach out to follow up on our previous conversation..."
- **Uses preview well:** "Shaw — quick one about the reporting workflow you mentioned..."

### 6. Match the commitment level to the relationship

Don't ask for a 60-minute meeting if a 1-sentence reply will do. The CTA should feel proportional to where the relationship is.

- **Post-call warm lead:** "Want me to send the intake?" (they already know the offer)
- **Past client:** "Worth a 15-minute catch-up?" (low friction, no new context needed)
- **Ghosted prospect:** "Still on your radar, or should I stop bugging you?" (gives them permission to say no, which often surfaces a yes)

## Structure (default — ultra-lean)

Most warm sales emails should be 3–5 short paragraphs max. Structure:

1. **Opener that uses recent context** (1 line).
2. **The specific thing you're following up on / offering** (1–2 lines).
3. **The ask — a question they can answer in a reply** (1 line).
4. **Sign-off** (— Shaw).

If your email is longer than this, ask what's doing the extra work.

## Which universal principles matter most here

- **#5 Cut ruthlessly** — warm emails live or die on brevity.
- **#6 Write how you talk** — conversational tone, contractions, short sentences.
- **#7 Don't persuade** — advice-framed outreach consistently outperforms pitch-framed.
- **#2 Use the customer's voice** — quote their words back to them when relevant.

## Voice cues

- First-name basis is fine (assumed from warm context).
- Don't formally sign — "— Shaw" beats "Best regards, Shaw Talebi."
- No subject-line gimmicks. "Quick follow-up" or "Your intake" or "[Name] — one question" work.
- Read it aloud. If it sounds like you're dictating a formal letter, rewrite.

## Worked example — post-call follow-up

**Context:** Shaw had a discovery call with Karen, a founder-CEO who's interested in the 30-day 1:1 intensive. She said on the call she wanted to think about it.

**Weak draft:**
> *Hi Karen,*
>
> *I hope this email finds you well. I wanted to reach out to follow up on our conversation from earlier this week. As I mentioned during our call, my 30-day AI intensive is designed to help founder-CEOs like yourself unlock the power of AI and transform your business operations.*
>
> *I would love to answer any questions you may have and discuss how we can work together to achieve your goals. Please let me know if you have availability for a follow-up conversation.*
>
> *Best regards,*
> *Shaw*

**Filter check:** First line burns preview. Landing page words everywhere. No specific reference to the call. Generic CTA. The whole message is neutral — could have been sent to anyone.

**Strong draft:**

> Karen — you mentioned the three hours of reporting every Friday is what's killing you. I've been thinking about what that looks like if we tackle it first in the 30-day intensive.
>
> Want me to send a 5-minute Loom walking through what Week 1 would look like specifically for your reporting workflow? No call needed to watch it.
>
> If it lands, you can reply "in" and I'll send the intake.
>
> — Shaw

**Why this works:**
- Uses preview text ("Karen — you mentioned the three hours of reporting...").
- References specific, verifiable call context.
- The "I've been thinking" signals actual thought, not a template.
- Offers a next step that's valuable even if she doesn't buy (a free Loom she can forward to her team).
- Two-step CTA (watch the Loom, then reply "in"). Lower friction than "let's schedule a call."
- Short. Direct. Doesn't rehash the sell from the call.

## Common failure modes

1. **"I hope this email finds you well" opener.** Burns preview text and signals a template.
2. **Re-pitching what was already pitched on the call.** If you sold it on the call, don't re-sell — address objections or lower the friction to the next step.
3. **"Let me know if you have any questions."** This is a dead close. Replace with a specific question.
4. **Long emails to post-call warm leads.** They already know the offer. Say the specific new thing, then stop.
5. **Formal sign-offs.** "Best regards, Shaw Talebi" reads as a form letter. "— Shaw" reads as a person.
6. **CTA asking for another call when a reply would do.** Don't escalate commitment unless you have to.
