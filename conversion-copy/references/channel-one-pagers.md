# Channel: One-Pagers

Use this framework when writing a one-pager — a single-page PDF, Notion doc, or Google doc that sells an offer. One-pagers are what Shaw sends to warm leads after a call, drops into a DM to qualify interest, or uses as the artifact behind a link in outreach.

One-pagers are the compressed cousin of landing pages. Same DNA, less real estate. Every section earns its place or gets cut.

## Before you draft

Lock these:

- **Who's reading it?** A warm lead post-call (high context — short and punchy) or a cold lead (needs more setup)?
- **What's the single CTA?** Book a call, reply, pay, apply.
- **What's the offer?** Container + outcome + timeframe + price.
- **Customer-language source.** (Ask if not provided.)

## Core principles (one-pager–specific)

### 1. Single primary CTA

Landing pages can sometimes afford two CTAs (one high-commitment, one low). One-pagers cannot. Pick one next step and make everything else serve it.

### 2. Proof beats promises

Warm leads reading a one-pager have usually already heard your pitch. They don't need the pitch re-sold — they need proof. Weight the page toward:

- Named clients (with permission).
- Specific outcomes with numbers.
- Screenshots of the work you did, not hypothetical diagrams.
- Testimonials pulled verbatim from happy customers.

If you're describing instead of showing, you're probably wasting space.

### 3. Lead with the unsignable angle

Same rule as landing pages, even sharper on a one-pager. The first block has to make the reader think *"this is specifically for me"* or *"only this person does this."* If they could swap your name out for a competitor's, start over.

### 4. Every section earns its place

If a section doesn't move the reader toward the CTA, cut it. Common deadweight on one-pagers:

- "About us" paragraphs nobody reads.
- Generic "why AI matters" preambles on AI-education one-pagers.
- Process steps that don't actually differentiate the offer.
- Bonus lists where each bonus doesn't clearly solve a next-problem-after-buying.

### 5. Repackage, don't just rewrite

Harry on the Haako rewrite: *"You can't expect people to read one huge text block to understand your product."* Often the fix is formatting, not words — condense a paragraph into one bold line plus a supporting image.

## Structure (default — lean version)

1. **Title + subhead** — outcome + container + timeframe in 2 lines.
2. **Who this is for** — 3–5 bullets, reader self-identifies.
3. **The promise** — one paragraph, value-equation-shaped (dream outcome / likelihood of success / time / effort).
4. **What you get** — deliverables + bonuses, bullet form.
5. **Proof** — 1–2 named client results + 1 pulled testimonial.
6. **Investment + CTA** — price, one-line scarcity, single action.

If Shaw wants the full 7-section sales-letter structure (including guarantee, process, longer proof sections), hand off to `sales-letter-writer`. This skill handles the sentence-level craft either way.

## Which universal principles matter most here

- **#4 Specific beats vague** — one-pagers live or die on specifics. Everything measurable gets measured.
- **#5 Cut ruthlessly** — one-pager means *one page.* If it's running to two, cut, don't shrink the font.
- **#1 Write for one reader** — a one-pager is usually being sent to a specific person. Write to them.
- **#10 Memorable beats likeable** — the one-pager is the artifact they forward to their co-founder. Make it forwardable.

## Voice cues

- If Shaw is sending this post-call to a specific person, it's OK to break the "don't address the reader by name in copy" rule — a one-pager attached to a DM can open with "Hey [name], here's what we talked about."
- Bullet form is the friend of the one-pager. Most of the page should be scannable.
- The price should never be buried. Either disclosed prominently or deliberately framed ("priced based on scope — let's jump on a 15-min call").

## Worked example — one-pager title + subhead for an AI intensive

**Weak:**
> *AI for Founders: Transform Your Business with the Power of Artificial Intelligence*
> *A premium coaching program for forward-thinking business leaders.*

**Filter check:** All three questions fail. Signable by anyone.

**Rewrite (three angles):**

1. **Outcome-led:**
   > *Reclaim 20–40 hours a month by Q3.*
   > *A 30-day 1:1 intensive for founder-CEOs who still approve every invoice — and want AI to take back the work they shouldn't be doing.*

2. **Competitor-led:**
   > *You don't need a $60k AI consulting engagement. You need 30 days and a plan.*
   > *A 1:1 intensive where we build your first AI automations together — no dev team, no SaaS subscription you'll forget about.*

3. **ICP-led:**
   > *Most AI training is written for engineers. This one isn't.*
   > *30 days, 1:1 with Shaw, built for owner-operators who run the business, not the codebase.*

Each version leads with a different angle and passes the 3-question filter. Top pick depends on traffic source — the competitor-led version hits hardest for warm leads who've been burned before; the outcome-led version works better cold.

## Common failure modes

1. **Page two creep.** If the one-pager is two pages, it's failed its format. Cut.
2. **Generic "about Shaw" paragraph.** Nobody cares about Shaw's background more than they care about what he'll do *for them.* Replace with proof.
3. **Undifferentiated bonuses.** Every bonus should name which next-problem-after-buying it solves. "Bonus: 30-day email support" is weak. "Bonus: 30 days of async messaging access — because the AI setup will break in week 2 and you'll need someone to debug it" is strong.
4. **No CTA, or a weak CTA.** "Let me know if interested" is a dead CTA. "Reply 'in' and I'll send you the intake form" is alive.
