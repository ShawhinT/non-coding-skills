# Tactical tips — lookup reference

This is a **lookup bucket**, not a primary check. Lean on `universal-principles.md` and the 3-question filter in `SKILL.md` first. Only consult this file when you're stuck on a specific tactical problem (a sentence that won't click, a CTA that feels flat, a hook that isn't landing) and want to see if a known tactic applies.

The tips below are grouped by problem you might be trying to solve. Scan the headings, not the whole file.

---

## Cutting & compression

Use when: the draft feels bloated, too long, too slow.

- **Ctrl+F for adverbs and adjectives.** Each one is a place where you hid from a specific claim. "Really effective" = "saves 10 hours a week." "Very fast" = "7-day turnaround." Replace each vague modifier with a number, proper noun, or concrete detail.
- **Cut the first sentence.** Your first sentence is almost always a warm-up. The second sentence is where the real copy starts. Try deleting the opener and see if the piece gets better.
- **Kill transitional phrases.** "With that said," "having said that," "moving forward," "at the end of the day" — these are throat-clearing. Readers don't need the connective tissue you do while writing.
- **Remove "that" wherever the sentence still works.** "The thing that we built" → "the thing we built." Roughly 30% of "thats" are removable.
- **One idea per sentence. One topic per paragraph.** If a sentence has an "and" joining two independent thoughts, it's two sentences pretending to be one.
- **Delete anything that could apply to your competitor.** If the line doesn't distinguish you, it's taking up space where differentiation could live.
- **Read aloud. Cut where you stumble.** If you trip over a phrase reading it out loud, the reader will trip too. Cut or rewrite.

## Voice & conversational register

Use when: the copy sounds stiff, corporate, or AI-written.

- **Use contractions.** "It's" not "it is." "Don't" not "do not." Copy without contractions reads like a legal document. Copy with contractions reads like a person.
- **Use "you" more than you think.** "We built this for small teams" → "If you run a small team, this is for you." Direct address creates presence.
- **Write in fragments sometimes.** Complete sentences aren't mandatory. Short fragments. Like this. They create rhythm and emphasis.
- **Start sentences with "and," "but," "so," or "because."** Your English teacher lied. Starting sentences with conjunctions makes copy feel conversational.
- **Swap "utilize" for "use."** Swap "leverage" for "use." Swap "facilitate" for "help." Multi-syllable verbs are usually hiding something plain.
- **Avoid "landing page words."** Seamless, robust, innovative, cutting-edge, transformative, revolutionary, forward-thinking, solutions, best-in-class, empowering. Every one of these makes the copy more forgettable, not more impressive.
- **If a 10-year-old can't follow it, simplify.** If a 10-year-old would find it boring, make it more specific. Both together.
- **Read it aloud one more time.** If it sounds like a speech a politician would give, rewrite.

## Momentum & openings

Use when: the piece starts slow, or the hook isn't earning the scroll/click.

- **Start with tension, not context.** "Most AI workshops teach you to set up a Python environment. My clients don't have a Python environment" beats "Hi everyone, today I want to share a thought about AI training."
- **Use specific numbers in the hook.** "40 hours" > "a lot of time." "$60k" > "a lot of money." Numbers create visual, falsifiable claims.
- **Use proper nouns.** "Barcelona, Juventus, PSG" > "top football clubs." "Last Thursday" > "recently." Specificity in the first sentence signals real content is coming.
- **Open mid-scene.** "She called me on a Friday at 9 PM." Drop the reader into a moment. Save the explanation for later.
- **Lead with a confession or a contrarian claim.** "I've done this wrong four times." / "Most AI workshops are built for engineers." Both hook because they break pattern.
- **Don't set the context before the hook.** The context can come in paragraph 2 once the reader is already pulled in.
- **The first sentence should make the second necessary.** If someone could stop reading after sentence one and not feel like they're missing anything, the opening hasn't earned the scroll.

## Stories & concrete examples

Use when: the copy feels abstract, theoretical, or like a list of claims without proof.

- **If you have a client anecdote, lead with it.** Anonymized is fine. "Last week a founder told me..." > "Many founders struggle with..."
- **Name names when you have permission.** "Karen from Austin" > "one of my clients." Specificity creates trust.
- **Use real dollar amounts and timeframes.** "Shaved 14 hours off her weekly admin load" beats "saved significant time." Verifiable numbers make you fall-from-the-sky credible.
- **Show the artifact, don't describe the outcome.** If you have the screenshot, the before/after, the DM — show it. "Here's the email she sent me after week one" is better than any summary of that email.
- **Paste the verbatim quote.** Don't paraphrase customer voice. Quote it exactly. Grammar errors and all — those errors prove it's real.
- **One story per piece.** Don't stack three client examples trying to prove a point. Pick the strongest one. Tell it well.

## CTAs & closes

Use when: the CTA feels flat, the close isn't closing, or the next step is too big a jump.

- **Match commitment to relationship.** Cold: reply-friction only. Warm: reply or low-time. Hot: book the call. Don't ask for a 60-minute call on first exposure.
- **Reply-prompts beat link-click prompts.** "Reply 'in' and I'll send the intake" converts higher than "click here to book." This is counterintuitive but consistent across channels.
- **Comment-prompts on LinkedIn also boost reach.** "Comment 'operator' and I'll DM you the one-pager" works for three reasons: low friction, signals intent, and the platform rewards comments.
- **Kill "Let me know if you have questions."** That's a dead close. Replace with a specific question they can answer in a word.
- **Don't stack CTAs.** One ask per piece. "Reply to this OR book a call OR reply to my LinkedIn OR fill this form" converts worse than any single CTA alone.
- **The P.S. is a second CTA in disguise.** Email readers who skim often read the P.S. even when they skipped the body. Use it. Don't waste it on a signoff.
- **Make "no" easy.** "Still on your radar, or should I stop bugging you?" gives permission to opt out. The people who don't opt out are the ones who were secretly interested.
- **Specify the outcome of saying yes.** "Reply 'in' and I'll send the intake" tells them exactly what happens next. Vague CTAs ("let's chat soon") lose because the reader doesn't know what they're agreeing to.

## Objections & honesty

Use when: you sense the reader has objections you're not addressing, or the copy feels like it's hiding from something.

- **Name the biggest objection in the copy.** If the price looks high, say so — then justify. If the time commitment is real, say so — then reframe. Dodging objections makes them louder, not quieter.
- **"This is probably not for you if..." sections build trust.** Disqualifying the wrong buyer paradoxically attracts the right one. It signals confidence.
- **Anti-hype is a positive signal.** "This isn't a silver bullet" or "you'll still have to do the work" reads as credible to sophisticated ICPs (founder-CEOs, operators). Hype reads as desperate.
- **If you make a claim, you need the proof next to it.** "We save clients 40 hours a week" without a testimonial, a screenshot, or a named example is wallpaper. Every big claim needs a proof point.
- **Don't promise what you can't deliver.** "Transform your business in 7 days" reads as a red flag unless you have the case study to match. Smaller, verifiable promises beat bigger, unverifiable ones.

## Format & scannability

Use when: the piece reads fine but feels visually overwhelming, or inversely, feels flat on the screen.

- **Short paragraphs.** 1–3 lines each. White space is a readability tool. Long walls of text lose readers regardless of the prose quality.
- **One idea per paragraph.** If you have to break the paragraph to insert a new idea, make it a new paragraph.
- **Bold sparingly.** If everything is bold, nothing is. Bold is for the one phrase you want someone skimming to catch.
- **Use bullet lists only when the items are genuinely parallel.** If three items don't share structure, prose reads better than bullets.
- **Put the most important line on its own line.** Orphaned sentences stand out. Use this for the single line you want to land hardest.
- **Headers are promises.** Every header should make the reader curious about the paragraph underneath. Generic headers ("About us," "Our process") waste the real estate.

## When everything feels off

When the piece isn't working and you can't point at a single tactical issue:

1. **Ask what you're really trying to say.** Often the draft is circling around the actual idea without landing on it. Write down the single sentence the piece exists to communicate. If you can't, you don't have the piece yet.
2. **Cut the middle.** If the first 20% and last 20% are strong, the middle is probably padding. Ruthlessly compress it.
3. **Rewrite from scratch without looking at the original.** 10 minutes. Timer on. You'll keep what mattered and shed what didn't.
4. **Show one real person.** Send the draft to someone in the target ICP. Their reaction tells you more than any tactical checklist.

## How to use this file

You generally shouldn't open this file as part of a normal review. Start with the 3-question filter and `universal-principles.md`. If a piece passes the filter but still feels off, or if you're trying to fix a specific sentence-level problem, scan the relevant heading here for a tactical nudge.

The universal principles tell you *what* to aim for. This file gives you *moves* when you're stuck.
