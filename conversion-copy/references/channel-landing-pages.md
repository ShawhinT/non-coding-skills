# Channel: Landing Pages

Use this framework when writing or reviewing a landing page — a standalone web page whose job is to convert a visitor into a lead, trial, or buyer. This includes offer pages, course pages, waitlist pages, launch pages, and product homepages.

Landing pages have the highest bar for copy craft because the visitor is on a 30-second speed date. Most of them won't return.

## Before you draft

Lock these:

- **Traffic type.** Cold (never heard of you) or warm (from a newsletter, referral, or search)? This changes the opening.
- **One job.** What's the single conversion action? Book a call? Buy? Join the waitlist? Everything on the page serves that one goal.
- **Reader's awareness level.** Do they know they have the problem, or do you need to name it for them?
- **Customer-language source.** See SKILL.md non-negotiable #1.

## Core principles (landing page–specific)

### 1. The title is the 30-second speed date

The majority of visitors look at the page for 30 seconds and never come back. If you can't make your product interesting in six words, sell the benefit instead.

- Write 10 titles. Show them to friends. Ignore their advice. Wait 24 hours. Ask which one they remember. That's your title.
- Memorable beats likeable. Weird beats smooth. Specific beats clever.
- The title must pass the central filter: visualize ✓, falsify ✓, unsignable ✓.

### 2. Speak with conviction

Soft language erodes trust. Own the claim.

- Don't say "We help" → say "It's how."
- Don't say "alternative" → say "replaces."
- Don't say "can" → say "does."
- Don't say "might" → cut it.

### 3. Match the reader's awareness level

Different visitors arrive with different context. One-size-fits-all copy converts worse than copy tuned to who's actually reading.

- **Warm leads arriving from a personal recommendation or newsletter** know the problem. Lead with the *solution*. ("Ace the SAT in 30 days — $1 trial.")
- **Cold leads arriving from organic search or a blog** don't know they have the problem yet. Lead with the *problem*. ("Your marketing dashboard is lying to you. Here's why.")

Ask Shaw which type of traffic this page will receive and structure accordingly.

### 4. Lead with what makes you unsignable

If your product has a unique angle, don't bury it halfway down the page. Lead with it.

- Ladybird Driving School: all instructors are female. Burying that at row 4 is malpractice — it's the *reason* people pick them.
- Socios: works with Barcelona, Juventus, PSG. Lead with the football clubs, not the "token" mechanic.
- For Shaw: the unsignable angle is usually *"built for operators, not engineers"* or *"the founder-CEO who still approves every invoice"* or *"no code, no dev team, works by end of day Friday."*

**Write the title only you can.**

### 5. Call-to-value, not call-to-action

Buttons that amplify *value* beat buttons that name the *action*.

- "Create Your Website" beats "Sign Up Now."
- "Get My Custom Plan" beats "Submit."
- "Book My Workshop Slot" beats "Schedule a Call."
- The CTA should echo the primary benefit.

### 6. Break long blocks into appetising chunks

People don't read paragraphs. They scan.

- *"3 simple steps"* converts better than *"two long paragraphs."*
- Use rows with alternating images + text. Bullet the deliverables. White space is free.
- Every section should be scannable in 3 seconds.

### 7. Social proof placed at objection points

Don't dump all testimonials in one block. Place them where readers are about to object.

- After the price → testimonial about ROI.
- After the process → testimonial about how easy it was.
- After the outcome claim → testimonial with the actual number.

### 8. Objection-led copy blocks

For each major objection ("I don't have time," "it's too expensive," "AI tools haven't worked for me"), have a section that directly addresses it. Don't force the reader to guess.

## Structure (default)

Use this as a starting skeleton unless Shaw has something specific in mind:

1. **Title + subhead** (above the fold)
2. **Who this is for** (qualifier — reader self-identifies)
3. **The promise** (outcome, mechanism, timeframe)
4. **Social proof** (logos, testimonials, numbers)
5. **How it works** (3 simple steps)
6. **What you get** (deliverables + bonuses)
7. **Guarantee** (risk reversal)
8. **Price + scarcity + CTA**

For long sales-page structure (full 7-section walk with formulas for each), use `sales-letter-writer`. This skill sharpens each section's sentences.

## Which universal principles matter most here

All 10 apply, but the biggest levers for landing pages:

- **#3 Sell outcomes, not features** — Dormio tea is the canonical example.
- **#4 Specific beats vague** — Counterweight Creative rewrite: "We replaced all their vague words with specific ones. You can't bullshit specifics."
- **#9 Create momentum** — every section must pull down to the next.
- **#10 Memorable beats likeable** — Jobs' Think Different.

## Voice cues specific to this channel

- First person plural ("we") is fine if Shaw has a team. First person singular ("I") is sharper for solo-operator offers.
- CTAs should sound like something the *reader* would say, not something a marketer would say. "Get me started" beats "Begin onboarding."
- The H1 should pass the read-aloud test. If it sounds like a SaaS headline generator wrote it, it needs surgery.

## Worked example — rewriting a weak landing page section

**Original (weak):**
> *Our AI workshops help forward-thinking leaders unlock unprecedented productivity gains by leveraging the latest in generative AI to supercharge their team's output and stay ahead of the curve.*

**Filter check:**
- Visualize? ✗ Can't see "productivity gains," "generative AI," "ahead of the curve."
- Falsify? ✗ No specific claim. Nothing to prove or disprove.
- Unsignable? ✗ Could come from any AI training company on earth.

**Rewrite (hitting the filter + outcome + customer voice):**
> *Three months from now, your team automates the busy work you hate — no code, no dev team, no $60k SOW. You spend the saved hours doing what you started the business to do.*

**Why this works:**
- Visualize? ✓ "Your team automating busy work." "You doing what you started the business to do."
- Falsify? ✓ "Three months." "No code." "No dev team." Falsifiable claims.
- Unsignable? ✓ "$60k SOW" names a specific competitor (bloated dev shops). Only Shaw's category of educator says this.
- Stacks multiple hooks: names the competitor ("$60k SOW"), neutralizes the objection ("no code"), paints the outcome ("doing what you started the business to do").

## Common failure modes

1. **H1 that any competitor could sign.** If Andrew Ng, Maven, or a random AI course creator could put their logo on it, rewrite.
2. **Features listed without the "so that."** Deliverables that don't translate to reader outcomes.
3. **CTA buttons with meaningless verbs.** "Submit," "Continue," "Go" — all losing to "Create my plan," "Book my workshop," "Start my intake."
4. **All testimonials in one block at the bottom.** They should be scattered next to the objections they kill.
5. **Landing-page words.** Unleash/unlock/empower/etc. appearing anywhere. Ctrl+F these before publishing.
