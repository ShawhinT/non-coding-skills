# Channel: Newsletters (meant to sell)

Use this framework when writing a newsletter whose job is to drive conversion — a launch announcement, a promotion email to Shaw's list, a re-engagement sequence, or the sell-side portion of a mixed newsletter.

This is **not** the framework for top-of-funnel content or teaching-focused essays. Those belong in `hormozi-content-framework`. Use this one only when the newsletter is selling a specific offer.

## Before you draft

Lock these:

- **Where is the list in the funnel?** Fresh list / warm regulars / dormant re-engagement? Each has a different opening move.
- **One offer per email.** What's the single thing being sold? Never two.
- **Expected open rate.** If the list isn't converting on opens, the subject line is the problem. If it opens but doesn't click, the body is.
- **Customer-language source.** Newsletters benefit enormously from customer quotes inside the body.

## Core principles (newsletter-specific)

### 1. Subject line: trust beats hype

The canonical example is Growth Design's 60%+ open rate newsletter. Their subject line: *"New: 🍿 Case Study #052"*. No clickbait. No exclamation marks. No urgency theater. Just the consistent promise they've delivered 52 times before.

- **For an established list:** subject line consistency is your highest lever. A recognizable pattern ("This Week: X") outperforms fresh cleverness.
- **For a launch or sell email (where consistency doesn't apply):** pick a subject that passes all three filter questions. Specific over clever. Concrete noun over abstract concept.
- Avoid all caps, excessive punctuation, and "landing page words." They trigger spam filters and reader skepticism.

**Weak subject lines:**
- *"🚀 HUGE Announcement — Don't Miss Out!!"*
- *"Transform your business with AI today"*
- *"You won't believe what I just launched"*

**Strong subject lines:**
- *"5 spots open — founder-CEO workshop"*
- *"The $60k mistake I keep seeing"*
- *"What Marcus saved by switching"*

### 2. First line determines whether they keep reading

Gmail and Apple Mail show the first 60–100 characters as preview text. Those characters decide whether the email gets opened or archived.

- Skip greetings that eat your preview. "Hi friends," is wasted real estate.
- Open with a scene, a claim, or a question that creates tension.
- If the email has a hook, put it in the first line — don't save it for paragraph 3.

### 3. Advice-email structure beats pitch-email structure

Sell emails that read like ads get deleted. Sell emails that read like valuable advice from a trusted operator get forwarded.

The "advice email" pattern (AndCo saw 65% CTR, 55% reply rate with this):
1. Valuable content first (teach, share an insight, tell a useful story).
2. Natural segue into the offer.
3. The offer as the next-step for readers who want more.
4. Low-friction CTA.

The reader finishes the email feeling like they *got* something — and that earns the right to ask for something.

### 4. One offer per email

Never pitch two things. Never mention a secondary "oh, and also." The email has one job. Every line serves it.

### 5. Conversational, personal tone

Newsletters are the most direct of the inbox channels. Write like you're emailing one person, not broadcasting to a list.

- "Hey" over "Hi all" or "Dear subscribers."
- Contractions throughout.
- Signature as a real name, not a brand.
- If you're in the inbox, be a person. Brands don't belong in the inbox.

### 6. P.S. is the second CTA

Readers who skim to the end of an email often read the P.S. even when they don't read the body. Use it to:

- Restate the CTA in different language.
- Add a piece of social proof.
- Surface the scarcity.

Don't waste the P.S. on a signoff.

### 7. Valuable before paid

The best sell newsletters spend 70%+ of their word count on value the reader gets from reading alone. The offer is the 30% tail. If the ratio is reversed, the list stops opening.

## Structure (default — advice-email pattern)

1. **Opening scene / hook** — specific, concrete, draws the reader in.
2. **The insight or story** — valuable content, teaching something real.
3. **Natural segue** — "Here's why I'm sending this..." or "This is why I built..."
4. **The offer** — what it is, who it's for, what they get.
5. **CTA** — single action, low friction.
6. **P.S.** — restate CTA or add scarcity/proof.

## Which universal principles matter most here

- **#2 Use the customer's voice** — quote real customer words inside the newsletter body. It breaks the "this is marketing" frame.
- **#6 Write how you talk** — newsletters should sound like email, not like web copy. Contractions, conjunctions, read-aloud test.
- **#9 Create momentum** — subject line earns the open, first line earns the second, body earns the CTA.
- **#7 Don't persuade** — advice format > pitch format.

## Voice cues

- The newsletter should feel like it came from a specific human, not a marketing system.
- Anti-hype language is a positive signal to Shaw's ICP. "This is probably not for you if..." sections work surprisingly well.
- Avoid perfect prose. A typo or a mid-sentence restart can make the email feel more personal.

## Worked example — launch email for Shaw's AI workshop

**Subject line:**
> *The founder who got her Saturdays back*

**Weak draft:**
> Hi everyone,
>
> I'm thrilled to announce that my AI workshop for forward-thinking founder-CEOs is now open for registration!
>
> In this comprehensive 2-day intensive, you'll learn how to leverage the latest AI tools to supercharge your business. We'll cover cutting-edge use cases, practical implementation strategies, and more.
>
> Spots are limited, so don't miss out! Click here to register today.
>
> Best,
> Shaw

**Strong draft (advice-email pattern):**

> Karen emailed me last month asking the same question three founders had already asked that week:
>
> *"I know I should be using AI but every time I sit down to learn it I end up watching 40 minutes of YouTube and closing my laptop more confused than I started."*
>
> Here's what I've noticed: the problem isn't the AI. It's that most AI training is written for engineers. Founder-CEOs don't need a Python tutorial. They need someone to sit with them for two days and build the three automations that actually move the needle in their business.
>
> So that's what I built.
>
> 2 days, in-person, 5 founder-CEOs in the room. No code. No lectures. By Friday afternoon you walk out with three AI automations running on your actual business — the ones eating your week right now.
>
> Karen's workshop cohort starts May 14. Five spots. Three are already gone.
>
> If you want the one-pager, reply "operator" and I'll send it.
>
> — Shaw
>
> P.S. — Last cohort, one founder shaved 14 hours off her weekly admin load inside the first week. If that sounds like something you'd like back, the link is above.

**Why this works:**
- Subject line paints a specific outcome ("got her Saturdays back"), no hype words.
- Opens with a real scene (scene passes filter — visualize ✓, falsify ✓, unsignable ✓).
- Advice structure — teaches before selling.
- Customer's voice quoted verbatim (Karen's email).
- Names the real enemy (engineer-focused AI training).
- Scarcity is specific and verifiable (5 spots, 3 gone).
- CTA is low-friction ("reply 'operator'").
- P.S. carries specific proof (14 hours, first week).

## Common failure modes

1. **Clickbait subject lines for an engaged list.** Regular readers who trust you don't need to be tricked into opening.
2. **"Hi everyone" / "Hi friends" openers.** They waste preview text and feel mass-mailed.
3. **Multiple offers in one email.** The "oh and also" kills conversion on both.
4. **Signoff with "Best" or "Warm regards."** Shaw's voice signs off "— Shaw" or similar. Corporate signoffs feel off.
5. **CTA that asks for a call.** Lower the bar — "reply with X," "hit this link," "forward this to someone who needs it."
6. **No P.S.** Leaving this section blank is leaving money on the table.
