# Harry Dry Swipe File

A curated library of examples from Harry Dry's Marketing Examples, organized by principle. Pull from this file when you need a proof-of-concept for a tactic, or when diagnosing copy and want to cite a parallel example.

---

## The 3-question filter in action

### New Balance — all three tests passing
> *"Worn by supermodels in London and dads in Ohio."*

- **Visualize:** The supermodel in London. The dad in Ohio with a barbecue. Both vivid.
- **Falsify:** Supermodels have worn New Balance. Dads in Ohio do wear them. Both true.
- **Unsignable:** Prada isn't worn by dads in Ohio. Supermodels don't wear Reebok. Only New Balance can say this.

### Weak example — recruitment company
> *"Don't just get a job. Change an entire industry."*

- Visualize: ✗ Can't picture "changing an industry."
- Falsify: ✗ Abstract claim.
- Unsignable: ✗ Any recruitment company could sign this.

## Concreteness / visualization

### Steve Jobs' Apple Lisa ad → Think Different
In 1983, Apple ran a nine-page ad in the New York Times for the Apple Lisa. Dense geek-speak. 10,000 units sold.

Fourteen years later, Jobs returned and ran a two-word billboard: *Think Different.*

No computers in the ads. No jargon. The campaign ran for 5 years and is credited with turning Apple's fortunes around. Principle: the nine pages of spec were abstract. Two words were concrete and memorable.

### Couch to 5K
Someone pitching a "fitness app to help people regain fitness" sounds forgettable. Zoom in: "regain" → "get up off the couch." "Fitness" → "running 5K." Couch to 5K. The most-downloaded fitness program of all time, built by a Boston TV producer, not Nike.

## Customer's voice

### Design Academy
Design Academy sends every new signup an email: *"What is your biggest frustration with design?"* The answers go into one big Google sheet.

Their landing page copy is pulled directly from that sheet — literally the phrases customers wrote. Laura from Design Academy: *"We didn't write that. Our customers wrote that. It was a recurring statement that we heard over and over and over again. All we did was listen."*

Takeaway: Shaw's equivalent is mining CRM notes, past client testimonials, LinkedIn/Reddit comments, and his own memory of what clients have said. The copy writes itself once the source material is in hand.

## Outcomes over features

### Dormio tea
**Before:** *A calming tea.*
**After:** *A calming tea that helps you relax, unwind, and drift into deep, restorative sleep.*

Takeaway: "tea" is a feature. "Drift into deep, restorative sleep" is the outcome the reader actually buys.

### Everydae (SAT prep)
**Before:** *The next generation of SAT prep.*
**After:** *Ace the SAT.*

Kids don't care about "next generation." They care about the outcome.

## Differentiation / unsignable

### Ladybird Driving School
All their instructors are female. But the original landing page buried this at row 4.

Fix: lead with it. "Women-only driving instruction." The *reason* people pick Ladybird was half-hidden on the original page.

Takeaway: *Write the title only you can.*

### Volvo / Volkswagen
Classic Volkswagen ad: *"Your car has five numbers on the speedometer. Volvo has six. One could get the impression that the people who made your car lack a little confidence."*

Unsignable. Only Volvo can say this. Requires actually counting the numbers on your competitor's speedometer.

### Socios
Footballer fans don't care about "tokens," "surveys," or "rewards." Socios' original page led with those terms.

Fix: lead with "fans want their voices heard" + Barcelona, Juventus, PSG as named partners. Visual proof + shared dream + named enemies of voice-less fan experience.

## Specificity

### Counterweight Creative rewrite
**Before:** Vague marketing language about "transformative results" and "strategic partnership."
**After:** *"We replaced all their vague words with specific ones. You can't bullshit specifics."*

Takeaway: Ctrl+F for adjectives and adverbs. Replace each with a specific claim.

## Conversational / voice

### Don't say "we help" — say "it's how"
Weak conviction: *"We help teams collaborate better."*
Strong conviction: *"It's how modern teams ship faster."*

Soft verbs erode trust. Conviction sells.

## Subject lines

### Growth Design — 60%+ open rate
Subject line: *"New: 🍿 Case Study #052"*

No clickbait. No urgency theater. Consistency and reputation do the work. 52 case studies earned the open — the subject line doesn't have to shout.

Takeaway: for an established audience, consistency > cleverness.

## Social / LinkedIn

### Kyiv Independent vs Kyiv Post
Both cover the Ukraine war. The Kyiv Post shares article links (redirecting readers off-platform). The Kyiv Independent condenses news into tweets and threads (giving value on-platform).

Start of war: Post had 10x more followers. Now: Independent has 5x more.

Takeaway: don't redirect. Be useful on the platform itself.

### Easy to sum up
*"If you want to grow on social, make yourself easy to sum up."*

If someone reading your post couldn't summarize it in one sentence to a friend, the post isn't sharp enough.

## Cold outreach

### Ryan's 1,000 replies
Ryan's framework for cold outreach that actually converts:

1. **Find hot leads** — scrape a tight cohort with shared needs, not broad lists.
2. **Turn leads into verified emails** — prospecting tools + deliverability checks.
3. **Get your email opened** — personalized first line = preview text = opens.
4. **Get a reply** — offer value upfront. Don't ask for a call. Ask the lowest-friction next question.

Closer: *"Find 100 people you can help. Offer to help them for free. Blow them away."*

### Dave Gerhardt's 1-line sales email
Example cold email to a CEO for an exec coaching program: a single line asking a question.

Goal: get a reply. A reply gives you something to work with. Much better than "quick call" templates going to 1,000 prospects.

### Advice email pattern (AndCo)
Pattern: ask for advice instead of pitching. AndCo's advice email saw **65% CTR, 55% reply rate.**

People love giving advice. It disarms them. Once they reply, you have permission to pitch.

## Landing page titles (Harry's five rules)

1. **Speak with conviction.** "It's how" not "we help."
2. **You're not Vladimir Nabokov.** Fancy titles that say literally nothing belong in failed poetry, not landing pages.
3. **You're on a speed date.** 30 seconds. Make it interesting in 6 words or sell the benefit.
4. **No universal approach.** Cold vs warm traffic need different titles. Annie Maguire (warm, recommendations) leads with solutions. Zenbu (cold, organic) leads with problems.
5. **The test.** Write 10 titles. Show friends. Ignore their advice. Wait 24 hours. Ask which one they remember.

## Quick-reference: Harry's three rules of copy

- Make it **concrete** (visualize).
- Make it **falsifiable** (specific, provable).
- Make it **unique** (unsignable).

Three yes → working. Any no → rewrite.
