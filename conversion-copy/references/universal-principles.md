# Universal Principles

Nine principles that apply across every conversion channel. These do the heavy lifting — lean on them before reaching for the ~50-tip tactical reference. The central 3-question filter (visualize / falsify / unsignable) checks individual lines; these principles shape the whole piece.

---

## 1. Write for one reader

You're not talking to 1,000 people. You're talking to the single person reading your page right now. Write like it.

- Use "you," not "users" or "customers" or "businesses."
- Narrow beats broad. "Founder-CEOs of 30-person professional services firms who still approve every invoice" converts better than "small business owners."
- Picture one specific customer by name when drafting. The right person reads it and thinks *that's literally me*.

**Bad:** *Our platform helps teams of all sizes work smarter.*
**Good:** *For the owner-operator who's the bottleneck for everything.*

---

## 2. Use the customer's voice

The best copy reads like the customer wrote it. Your job is closer to a journalist than a poet — you're reporting the phrases they already use, not inventing cleverness.

- Mine testimonials, CRM notes, Reddit, LinkedIn comments on similar offers, online forums, and Shaw's own memory of recent client conversations.
- If they say "drowning in busy work," write "drowning in busy work." Don't translate it into "experiencing operational overwhelm."
- Ask a new customer on signup: "What's your biggest frustration with X?" Save every answer. Those are your future headlines. (Design Academy did exactly this — their whole landing page is quotes from that signup question.)

**Bad:** *Experience unparalleled efficiency gains.*
**Good:** *I stopped being the bottleneck. That's the whole pitch.* ← actual customer quote

---

## 3. Sell outcomes, not features

Customers buy outcomes, not products. People don't want a better toothbrush — they want a brighter smile. Kids don't care about "next-generation SAT prep." They care about acing the SAT.

- Every feature bullet should translate to a reader benefit. If you can't finish the sentence *"...so you can ___"*, cut the feature.
- The outcome should be visceral and visualizable. *"Close your laptop at 5 and actually be done"* beats *"improved work-life balance."*
- Dormio: "A calming tea" → "A calming tea that helps you relax, unwind, and drift into deep, restorative sleep."

**Bad:** *AI-powered workflow automation for enterprise teams.*
**Good:** *Get 10 hours a week back by offloading your most repetitive work to AI.*

---

## 4. Specific beats vague

You can't bullshit specifics. Every time you reach for an adjective ("fast," "easy," "powerful"), replace it with a concrete fact.

- Numbers, named customers, concrete timeframes, exact deliverables.
- Kill adverbs and adjectives. They're flowery, vague, and they try too hard.
- "We replaced their vague words with specific ones" — Harry on landing page rewrites.

**Bad:** *Our clients see massive improvements in team productivity.*
**Good:** *Clients reclaim 20–40 hours of busy work per month within 30 days.*

---

## 5. Cut ruthlessly

Kaplan's Law: any word that isn't working for you is working against you. Harry: *"A great sentence is a good sentence made shorter."*

- Write with your eraser. You get $100 for every word you cut from a title.
- Delete qualifiers ("we might be able to," "could potentially help," "in order to"), setup phrases, adverbs, throat-clearing.
- More periods, fewer commas. Long sentences with nested clauses that wind on and on without actually saying anything feel clever to the writer and torturous to the reader. Periods are your friend.
- The first line is crucial. If people don't read it, they're not going to read the second line either. Keep it short.

**Bad:** *We think we might be able to help you potentially save time each week with our platform.*
**Good:** *Save 5 hours a week.*

---

## 6. Write how you talk

If you wouldn't say it out loud at a bar, don't write it. Read every line aloud. If your partner cringes, rewrite it. If you stumble, rewrite.

- Use contractions. Only academics say "you are."
- Start sentences with conjunctions. And it flows better. But yes, really.
- Don't worry about grammar rules that don't serve the reader. Breaking them makes you sound human.
- Ditch the thesaurus. You're not impressing anyone.
- Don't imitate. *"You're alive in inverse proportion to the density of clichés in your writing"* — Taleb.
- **Kill "landing page words."** Unleash, unlock, supercharge, empower, enhance, exceed, elevate, transform, revolutionary, game-changing, cutting-edge, seamless, robust, leverage-as-a-verb. Real people don't use them.

**Bad:** *Unlock unparalleled efficiency and supercharge your team's productivity.*
**Good:** *Your team stops doing the work they hate. That's it.*

---

## 7. Don't persuade — let them be persuaded

The harder you push, the more they resist. Instead of making claims, point at artifacts. Instead of pitching, offer advice.

- Show, don't tell. Don't say "we save you time." Show the before/after screenshot of the workflow. Point at the chart. Name the client and the number.
- Ask questions instead of making statements. "What if your team could close their laptops at 5 without the Monday dread?" invites agreement; declarations invite pushback.
- Advice-framed emails outperform pitch-framed emails (AndCo saw 65% CTR and 55% reply rate from an advice email vs a typical sales email).
- Respect the reader's intelligence. If the copy is right, they'll persuade themselves.

**Bad:** *Don't miss this revolutionary opportunity to transform your business.*
**Good:** *Here's what three founders did with AI last month. See if any of it applies.*

---

## 8. Create momentum

Every line of copy should pull the reader to the next. Harry calls it the "slippery slide." You're building a chute, not a wall.

- The first line is 80% of the job. If it doesn't earn the second line, nothing else matters.
- Avoid "contained" titles that stand alone. Write titles that make the reader *need* to read the next line.
- Find the tension. Pleasant copy gets skimmed. Conflict creates interest. "Fence sitters don't buy — go to the edge."
- No paragraph break should feel like an exit ramp.

**Bad (self-contained):** *We help you save time with AI.*
**Good (pulls you down the page):** *Here's what happened when three founders handed their inbox to an AI agent for a week.*

---

## 9. Memorable beats likeable

Clever-and-forgettable is worse than plain-and-sticky. Jobs shrank nine pages of Apple Lisa spec to two words on a billboard: *Think different.* Nobody remembered the spec. Everyone remembered "Think different."

- Harry's title test: write 10 titles, show them to friends, ignore their advice, wait 24 hours, ask which one they remember. That's your title.
- The goal isn't to get nods in the room. It's to be the thing they remember when they're ready to buy.
- Don't try too hard. Customers can see through fake shit.

**Bad:** *Elegantly architected AI solutions for the modern enterprise.*
**Good:** *AI for the operator, not the engineer.*

---

## How to use these principles

When **drafting**: hold them in your head as you write. They shape tone, voice, and positioning — not just individual sentences.

When **reviewing**: flag the top 2–3 violations, not all 9. The goal is to fix the copy, not to produce a report. The most common violations for Shaw's audience (founder-CEOs / owner-operators) are usually #2 (not enough customer voice), #4 (vague claims), and #6 (landing-page words creeping in).
