# Script body (motivation/story + outro)

How to write the section right after the intro and the section that closes the video. The intro promises a contract; the body delivers it; the outro keeps the viewer in motion.

## Convention for script bullets

In Shaw's script docs, each bullet under a section is **one spoken line** — what he reads aloud. Don't write paragraphs as bullets; break sentences out so each is its own line. The convention is consistent across Intro, Motivation, and Outro sections.

## Section 1: Motivation / story (right after the intro)

Comes immediately after the 3-line hook. Job: prove this video is for the viewer (relatability) and that you're qualified to deliver on the contract (credibility). ~30 seconds total.

### Three opening moves

Pick one. Each leads differently.

**A — Personal proof first, then story** (authority frame)
- Lead with credibility numbers (hours with Claude, hours delegated, clients coached)
- Then a specific client story that mirrors the avatar
- Then the transition into the body

**B — Story first, then proof** (retention frame, usually wins)
- Lead with the client story — let the viewer see themselves in the protagonist before knowing who you are
- Drop credibility as evidence after the story has hooked
- Transition into the body

**C — Curation play** (pattern-recognizer frame)
- Lead with "I've worked with X people and they all start the same way"
- Name the pattern
- Show one specific example
- Transition into the body

### Default to B for retention

Story-first is the highest-retention move on YouTube. The viewer needs to *see themselves* before they care who you are. By the time the proof hits, they're already invested.

A is the right move when the existing channel audience expects credibility upfront, or when the content is technical-deep and viewers need to know you're qualified before listening.

C is the right move when the avatar feels alone in their problem and you're positioning as the curator who has seen this pattern repeatedly.

### Sourcing the story

Use a real client when possible. Pull from `ABA Calls` (Notion) for relevant prospects/clients whose pain matches the video's topic. The story shape:

1. **Who they are.** Specific role + business stage. ("Founder-CEO of a mid-market fintech company.")
2. **Where they were.** Their state when they came to you. Use verbatim language they used if you have it.
3. **What was missing.** The specific gap that maps to the video's promise.
4. **What changed.** One concrete moment of inflection.
5. **Result.** Brief.

### Permissions and discretion

Before naming a client publicly:
- Confirm permission to use their name and company
- If unconfirmed, anonymize via role + industry + size ("the CEO of a 1,200-person company in Prague" or "a mid-market fintech CEO")
- Anonymized doesn't lose impact — the story shape carries the weight, not the name

### Bridge into the body

Last bullet of the motivation section should set up what comes next. Pattern: *"Here's exactly what we did in the [N]-minute session"* or *"I'll walk you through the framework I use with every client like this."* Earns the next section.

## Section 2: Outro (close the video)

The job is the opposite of what most people think. **Don't end the video.** If the viewer feels closure, they leave. The outro keeps them in motion.

### Ed Lawrence's "Don't end the video" principles

- **No recap.** They just watched it.
- **No "thanks for watching."** Signals end.
- **No "smash subscribe."** Doesn't drive action.
- **No questions with no payoff.**
- **End on a strong final point** from the body — feels like a mic drop, not a wrap-up.
- **Pivot to the next video** that deepens what they just learned.
- **Make the bridge logical** — "if you understood X, you're ready for Y."
- **Show, don't tell.** Let the end-screen visual do the work.
- **Keep it short.** ~10-15 seconds total.

### The outro structure

Three or four bullets, each one spoken line:

1. **Final insight / mic drop.** Often callbacks the title or intro. ("That's the one thing.")
2. **Bridge.** Names the gap between knowing and doing. ("But hearing me talk about it isn't the same as actually building one.")
3. **Specific next-video tease.** What they'll learn, why it matters. Reference the actual title of the next video if possible.
4. *(Optional)* "I'll see you there" or equivalent only if it adds energy. Often skip it.

### Bridge construction

The bridge is where the outro lives or dies. Three patterns:

**Knowing → doing**
> "Knowing skills exist isn't the same as building one. Watch this next."

**Concept → execution**
> "But hearing me talk about a framework isn't the same as watching one get built. Here's me building one from scratch."

**Single insight → broader system**
> "That's the one piece. The full system is in this video."

Pick based on what the next video actually delivers. Don't promise "from scratch" if the next video is conceptual; don't promise "the full framework" if the next video is one example.

### End-screen timing (production note)

The end-screen card with the next video drops on screen *as you say* "watch this next" — not after. Visual + verbal hit at the same moment, or you lose the click. Cut the recording the second the last sentence ends; any silence reads as "the video is over" and viewers leave before the click.

### When the next video doesn't exist yet

Two fallbacks:

1. **Resource pivot** — point to GitHub, a real artifact, or a course that already exists. "Every skill I've built is on GitHub. Watch this next to see how I built the one that runs my CRM." (Requires a real video on a specific build.)
2. **Workshop pivot** — point to the offer + a next video as a fallback. Not as Ed-pure, but works when the next video doesn't exist and the avatar is buyer-ready. Risk: two CTAs is one too many. Use sparingly.

### Sales pitch placement

The outro is *not* the place for a hard sales pitch unless the offer is the most logical next step for that exact viewer. Default to the next-video pivot. Sales lives in the description, the pinned comment, and the body of the video where you can soft-mention the offer in context.

## Iteration logistics

The order parallels the intro workflow.

### Motivation section
1. Confirm the avatar and the topic
2. Identify a real client whose pain matches the video's promise — pull from `ABA Calls`
3. Generate 2-3 framings (A, B, C above) so the user can pick the angle, not the words
4. Recommend B unless the channel audience expects A or the avatar profile justifies C
5. Once framing is locked, draft the actual script as bullets (one sentence per bullet)
6. Flag any client-permission or factual-defensibility risks (employee count, revenue, named individuals)

### Outro
1. Identify the actual next video the outro will point to. Real title.
2. Generate 2-3 outro variants with different bridge moves (knowing→doing / concept→execution / single→system)
3. Filter against what the next video actually delivers — don't promise format the next video doesn't have
4. Recommend the variant whose bridge most cleanly matches the next video's payoff
5. Add production notes (end-screen timing, no-pause cut)

## Output format (motivation)

```
**Section:** Motivation
**Goal:** Prove relevance + credibility, transition into body. ~30 seconds.
**Story source:** <which client / where pulled from>

**Three framings:**

A — Personal proof first
> [Bullets]

B — Story first
> [Bullets]

C — Curation play
> [Bullets]

**Recommendation:** <pick + reasoning>

**Permission/defensibility flags:** <any names, numbers, claims to verify before recording>
```

## Output format (outro)

```
**Section:** Outro
**Next video:** <actual title of the video this outro points to>
**Goal:** Keep the viewer in motion. ~10-15 seconds.

**Three variants:**

A — Knowing → doing bridge
> [Bullets]

B — Concept → execution bridge
> [Bullets]

C — Single → system bridge
> [Bullets]

**Recommendation:** <pick + reasoning>

**Production notes:**
- End-screen timing: <when the card drops>
- No silence after final line: <reminder>
```

## Common failure modes

- **Motivation section that's all you, no client.** Story carries retention; pure credibility doesn't.
- **Stacking 4+ credibility numbers.** One or two land. Three or more reads as bragging.
- **Outro that recaps the body.** Viewers leave the moment recap starts. Skip it.
- **Outro that ends with "thanks for watching."** Signals end. Cut it.
- **Bridge that promises format the next video doesn't deliver.** Promise carefully — the package is a contract.
- **Motivation that uses the same anchor as the intro.** If the intro mirrored the avatar's pain, the motivation should pivot to a *specific person* who lived that pain — same theme, different lens.
- **Double-stacking a `\[CARD\]` and `\[ANIMATION\]` on the same beat.** If a section card's glyph already carries the visual point, drop the inline animation at that line (or vice versa). One visual idea per beat.
- **Over-specifying visuals at the script level.** Inline `\[CARD\]` / `\[ANIMATION\]` callouts describe *what's on screen* (subject, glyph, label). Don't redefine type, palette, lighting, or motion language — that's the thumbnail's job and it propagates down.
