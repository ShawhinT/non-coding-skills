# Newsletter Writer

Shaw's canonical skill for drafting newsletters to his Kit list (The Data Entrepreneurs, ~5,000 subscribers, mostly founders, operators, and AI-curious professionals).

The skill is built on patterns extracted from his sent archive — the moves that show up in nearly every email he ships, in the rhythm and length that consistently performs.

## Before drafting

Lock these:

- **Mode**: content/teaching, observation, or sell? (Determines the template and the P.S.)
- **One core idea**: every newsletter has exactly one. If there are two, split it into two emails.
- **Source material**: post being repurposed, framework being taught, observation being shared, or offer being sold. If Shaw hands over a LinkedIn post, draft, or rough notes, lean on it heavily — his existing language is usually closer to the final voice than anything you'd invent.
- **Length target**: 250–450 words. If you're approaching 500, cut.

If any of the above is unclear, ask before drafting.

## Voice (non-negotiable)

Shaw's voice is a confident peer-to-peer operator. Smart, no-fluff, declarative. Not coaching, not hype, not corporate.

- **Cold open.** Never "Hey," "Hi friends," or any greeting. Open with the claim, the scene, or the problem.
- **One thought per line.** Short paragraphs, often single sentences. Heavy whitespace. The page reads like a stack of statements, not a wall of prose.
- **Declarative over coaching.** State the thing and move on. No "that's fine," "don't worry," or pep-talk reassurance.
- **Anti-hype.** Kill landing-page words: unleash, supercharge, transform, empower, leverage, revolutionary, game-changing, cutting-edge, next-gen, seamless, robust, elevate, exceed.
- **Visceral verbs.** "Delete 20 hours" beats "save 20 hours." "Hand off" beats "automate." "Kill the bottleneck" beats "reduce it."
- **Specific over vague.** Numbers, named roles, concrete examples. "165 alumni, 4.7-star rating" beats "lots of happy students." "The head of data at an enterprise" beats "some leaders."
- **Light on em-dashes.** Shaw has asked to ease off them. Use commas, periods, or parentheses. One or two per email is fine.
- **Confident first person.** Shaw uses "I" and "you" generously. He doesn't hide behind "we" or passive voice.

## Subject lines

Three patterns that perform in his archive. Pick whichever fits the mode.

**Curiosity gap (ellipsis)** — best for personal/announcement or contrarian takes. His top-performing subject style.
- *"Something's changed..."* (43% open)
- *"Why AI transformations fail..."* (27% open)
- *"You're Not Behind: What's (not) new in AI"*

**"How I / How to"** — best for content/teaching emails.
- *"How I Turn AI Ideas into Applications"*
- *"How to learn AI skills (that won't go obsolete)"*
- *"How I delegated 15 hrs/week to Claude"*

**Concrete contrast** — best for sell or strong-result emails.
- *"3 weeks of waiting, done in 1 hour"*
- *"If you only learn one AI skill, learn this."*

Avoid emojis in subject lines (rare in his archive — they belong in the P.S. or body if anywhere). Avoid all-caps, exclamation marks, urgency theater.

When uncertain between two subject lines, offer both with reasoning rather than pre-selecting.

## The two templates

### 1. Framework email (most common)

Used when teaching a process, set of lessons, or steps. ~70% of his content sends.

**Structure:**
1. **Cold open** — 1–3 short lines. Sets up the problem, the personal credibility, or the contrarian observation.
2. **Reframe / principle** — the "not X, but Y" pivot. Often called out with `└→` or `→`.
3. **Numbered steps or lessons** — usually 3–5. Each one: title + 1–3 short paragraphs.
4. **Close** — short summary line, optional question hook for replies.
5. **Signoff** — "Keep building, / Shaw"
6. **P.S.** — single CTA (free resource for content emails, paid offer for sell emails).

**Example openers (study the rhythm):**
- *"Over the past 18 months, I shipped my first two AI SaaS products. Not because I'm some cracked, 10X developer, but because I used AI coding tools (in the right way)."*
- *"I've quietly handed off about 15 hours of busywork a week to Claude."*
- *"Although building software is easier than ever, most AI projects still stall halfway through."*

### 2. Observation email

Used when sharing a trend, signal, or pattern from talking to people in the field. ~25% of his content sends.

**Structure:**
1. **Cold open** — names the trend or contrast.
2. **Setup** — "I've spent the past two weeks talking to..." or similar grounding line.
3. **Named-source examples** — usually 3 short paragraphs, each with a specific role + a specific thing they said or did.
4. **Implication** — what this means, often with a triplet of short parallel sentences.
5. **Question hook** (optional) — "What's one thing that has transformed for you?"
6. **Signoff** — "Keep building, / Shaw"
7. **P.S.** — relevant free resource or event.

**Example named-source proof:**
- *"The head of data at an enterprise said business stakeholders are now vibe-coding their own dashboards..."*
- *"A freelance analytics engineer built an agent skill teaching Claude how to interact directly with raw data sources..."*
- *"A data analyst at a mid-sized company used Claude to optimize an ML model..."*

The roles and contexts are specific. The proof is named, not abstract.

## Signature moves (use at least 2-3 per email)

These show up across nearly every newsletter in his archive. They're not optional flavor — they ARE the voice.

**1. The reframe ("Not X, but Y" / "From X → Y")**
The single highest-leverage move in his writing. Almost every email contains at least one.
- *"AI transformation isn't about bolting a new tool onto how you already work. It's about redesigning the work around what AI unlocks."*
- *"Last year, this would have taken hours of building custom AI automations. Today, you can do it all without a single line of code."*
- *"It went from 'how do I write this code?' → 'how do I get AI to write this code?'"*
- *"value is generated, not by creating software, but by solving problems"*
- *"instead of trying to predict an unpredictable future, reflect on the certain past"*

**2. Visual notation (`→`, `└→`, `>>`)**
Used as deliberate signals, not punctuation.
- `└→` for the principle/insight callout — *"└→ If you want to know what will last, look to what has lasted."*
- `→` for transitions inside a sentence — *"Equip → Educate → Adopt → Redesign"*
- `>>` for sequence/flow diagrams — *">> User provides input → App does something → App produces output"*

**3. Self-deprecating credibility**
Acknowledge what the reader might be thinking, then redirect. Lets you make a strong claim without sounding boastful.
- *"Not because I'm some cracked, 10X developer, but because I used AI coding tools (in the right way)."*

**4. "Pro Tip:" parenthetical asides**
Micro-callouts mid-body. Adds value without breaking flow.
- *"Pro tip: AI champions inside the team are a great way to foster this."*
- *"Pro Tip: The number of GitHub stars is a quick proxy for library quality."*

**5. Parenthetical emphasis**
Drop modifiers in parens to underline a word. Conversational, high-status.
- *"working (very) hard to make a modest impact"*
- *"(super) early bird"*
- *"(not) new in AI"*

**6. Triplet rhythm**
Three short parallel sentences as a beat. Use at the close or to land an implication.
- *"Create more dashboards. Validate business metrics faster. Build better analytical models."*
- *"No code. No new tools. No engineering background."*

**7. Specific numbers and named sources**
Always anchor claims. "165 alumni" beats "many alumni." "The head of data at an enterprise" beats "a leader."

## Close patterns

**Standard signoff (near-universal):**
```
Keep building,
Shaw
```

The new "How I delegated 15 hrs/week" used "-Shaw" instead — that's a departure. Default to "Keep building, / Shaw" unless Shaw explicitly asks otherwise.

**Optional question hook before signoff** when you want to drive replies:
- *"Which one of these lessons resonated with you? Hit reply and let me know :)"*
- *"What's one thing that has transformed in your analytics work because of AI?"*

Question hooks are cheap engagement — they warm the list without asking for a click.

## P.S. patterns (always present, always a CTA)

The P.S. is never empty. It's always a single CTA. Three flavors based on email mode:

**Free resource** (default for content/teaching emails):
- Link to a YouTube video that goes deeper
- Link to a free workshop or live event
- Link to a free course or guide

**Soft offer** (when nudging toward a paid product):
- Lead magnet that bridges to the paid offer
- Discount code with light scarcity

**Hard sell** (only for dedicated sell emails):
- Direct pitch with price, scarcity, and CTA link
- Used sparingly — most P.S. lines are free resources

The P.S. should restate or extend the value of the email, not introduce a totally new topic.

## Length

- **Content/teaching:** 250–450 words.
- **Observation:** 200–350 words.
- **Sell:** 300–500 words.
- **Personal/announcement:** can run longer (up to 600), but only when the personal narrative warrants it.

If the draft runs past 500 words on a content email, cut. Compression is a feature.

## Output 2-3 versions, not one

First-pass copy is rarely the best. When drafting:

- Offer 2–3 angle variants for the **opener** specifically (e.g., one leads with the problem, one with personal credibility, one with the contrarian reframe). State which you'd pick and why.
- For the rest of the email, one strong draft is fine — but call out alternate phrasings for any line that's load-bearing (the reframe, the close, the P.S.).
- Don't pre-select silently. Show your work so Shaw can iterate.

## Things to avoid

- "Hey friends," / "Hi everyone," — never. Cold open or nothing.
- "Best," / "Warm regards," — never. "Keep building, / Shaw" or similar.
- "Unleash," "supercharge," "transform" — landing-page words.
- Multiple offers in one email — never. One CTA per send.
- Empty P.S. — wasted real estate.
- Overusing em-dashes — Shaw has explicitly asked to ease off.
- Generic claims without numbers or named sources.
- Coaching tone ("don't worry," "you've got this") — Shaw doesn't reassure, he states.
- Walls of text — break up with whitespace, single-sentence paragraphs.
- Subject lines with all-caps, multiple exclamation marks, or "Don't miss out!" energy.

- **Deep diagnosis on a sell newsletter** → after drafting, hand off to `conversion-copy` (newsletter channel) for line-by-line filter checks (visualize / falsify / unsignable).
- **Top-of-funnel essays / Hormozi-style content** → `hormozi-content-framework` if Shaw explicitly wants the Mozi Minute structure (one idea + stories + reasons + soft CTA). Most of Shaw's newsletters are not Mozi-style — they're frameworks or observations.
- **LinkedIn posts (not newsletters)** → `linkedin-post-writer`.
- **General email drafts (not list broadcasts)** → `email-helper`.
- **Long-form sales letter for a paid offer** → `sales-letter-writer`.

## Worked example: repurposing a LinkedIn post into a Framework email

**Source post (raw):**
> I've delegated 15 hr/wk of busywork to Claude. My approach was surprisingly simple — no coding or new tools needed. It's a 4-step process: (1) Do it once with Claude (2) Distill into skill (3) Test it (4) Improve it.

**Newsletter draft (Framework template):**

> **Subject:** How I delegated 15 hrs/week to Claude
> **Preview:** Pro tip — treat Claude like an intern, not a tool.
>
> I've quietly handed off about 15 hours of busywork a week to Claude.
>
> This includes: email drafts, pre-call research, CRM updates, LinkedIn posts, outreach campaigns, lead qualification, and daily briefings.
>
> Last year, this would have taken hours of building custom AI automations.
> Today, you can do it all without a single line of code.
>
> The shift was treating Claude like an intern instead of a tool. In other words, thinking of the task as onboarding rather than engineering.
>
> Here's the 4-step loop I run through.
>
> **Step 1: Do it once (with Claude)**
> Walk Claude through the task with you in the loop.
> It'll take longer than doing it yourself, but this is an investment (just like training a new hire).
>
> **Step 2: Distill into Skill**
> Tell Claude to turn what you just did into a reusable skill.
> It writes its own SOP so you don't have to. This is important because Claude understands Claude better than we do.
>
> **Step 3: Test it**
> New chat. Fresh input. Run the skill.
> Should be 5x faster than Step 1, because Claude's running the SOP instead of figuring it out from scratch.
>
> **Step 4: Improve it**
> Skills aren't static. When Claude misses something, or you want a new capability, just tell it.
> This allows you to teach Claude about your work "little and often" rather than all at once.
>
> **Next Step:** Look at your calendar for the past 2 weeks and see what you're spending the most time on. Then pick 1 task and try to delegate some (or all) of it to Claude.
>
> If you get stuck, reply to this email and I'll point you in the right direction.
>
> Keep building,
> Shaw
>
> **P.S.** This is the exact 4-step loop we run inside my 1:1 Claude Workshops. This is the last round before they go public. 3 spots left, first-come, first-served. [Book your fit call →]([calendar-link])

**What this draft did well (in this skill's terms):**
- Cold open (no "Hey,").
- One thought per line.
- Compare-and-contrast pair: *"Last year [X]. Today [Y]."*
- Reframe: *"intern instead of a tool"* — names the shift cleanly.
- Numbered framework with title + 2 short lines per step.
- Pro Tip-style aside via the analogy in Step 1: *"(just like training a new hire)"*.
- "Little and often" — aphoristic punchline.
- Engagement layer (reply if stuck) before the offer P.S.
- P.S. is a single hard-sell CTA appropriate for this mode.
- "Keep building, / Shaw" signoff.

## One last thing

The newsletter is in the inbox. Brands don't belong in the inbox. Be a person.

If a draft sounds like a marketing system wrote it, rewrite it until it sounds like Shaw on a Tuesday morning, talking to one operator who already knows what he does.
