# Time study

The point: get an honest picture of how the user actually spends their week so you can spot what's worth automating. The calendar reveals scheduled blocks; the interview catches the work that never makes it onto a calendar.

The default mode is **synthesize first, then ask gap-fill questions** — not a generic question script. Generic questions blur together and make it easy to be lazy. Specific questions grounded in what you've already seen do real work.

Get through this in 5 minutes if you can. Longer than that and the user starts performing for you instead of telling you the truth.

---

## Calendar screenshot

**The ask:**

> Can you share a screenshot of a typical week on your calendar? Week view (not day view) — I want to see the shape of the week, not the detail of one day.

If the user is hesitant, name the reason: *"I'm looking for patterns — recurring meetings, time blocks, the rhythm. Not reading anything else."*

**What to look for:**

- **Recurring meetings.** Daily standups, weekly 1:1s, monthly reviews. Strongest signals — anything on a schedule is automatable-adjacent.
- **Time blocks.** "Deep work," "writing block," "Notion + email" — these tell you what the user *thinks* they spend time on. Useful for triangulating against what they say.
- **White space.** Big empty stretches usually mean reactive work (email, Slack, inbound) or unstructured creative work. Both interesting.
- **Color coding.** Dominant color tells you where the bulk of the week goes.
- **The shape.** Front-loaded (busy Mon-Wed) vs. evenly distributed vs. back-loaded — often correlates with role type and where automation lands best.

**What to ignore:**

- Specific names of meetings or attendees — privacy and not the point
- Exact times — round to ranges (morning / midday / afternoon / evening)

**If the screenshot looks sparse:** Don't jump to the no-calendar fallback. Many people run 2–3 calendars in parallel — work, personal, sometimes one per client. A sparse week is often just a single calendar showing. Ask: *"This week looks lighter than I'd expect — do you keep other calendars (personal, client-specific) you can turn on so I see the whole picture?"* Wait for the layered view. Reading a sparse single-calendar view will systematically miss the bulk of their week.

**No-calendar fallback:** If the user doesn't really use a calendar, don't push it. Add to your interview: *"Walk me through what yesterday actually looked like, hour by hour, as best you remember."* That's your calendar substitute.

## The interview — Mom Test framing

After the calendar (or in place of it), ask 2–4 *specific, recent, observable* questions tailored to gaps in your picture. Mom Test framing: ask about behavior that already happened, not what they think or feel in the abstract.

- ✅ *"When was the last time you got stuck doing X — what email, what made it slow?"*
- ❌ *"What's frustrating about your inbox?"*

- ✅ *"Walk me through what happened in the last Notion+email block on your calendar."*
- ❌ *"How do you usually handle email?"*

The questions should come from what you actually saw in Phase 0 + the calendar — not from a checklist. A question like *"Of all your skills, which 2–3 do you call most often, and do you ever call them in the same sequence back-to-back?"* is worth ten generic ones because it targets a real pattern in their data.

If you find yourself reaching for a generic question, stop and ask: *what specifically did I see that I want to understand better?* Lead with that.

## Fallback elicitation (when Phase 0 was thin)

Use this only when you don't have enough Phase 0 signal to ask targeted questions — typically L0/L1 users with no connectors and no session history. The principles, not a script:

- **Anchor to a real time window.** Don't ask about work in the abstract — ask them to look at their actual calendar from the last week or two. Memory is unreliable; calendar entries aren't.
- **Force a short ranked list.** "Top 3" beats "what's repetitive" — prioritization is built into the ask, and the cap stops the user from hedging into ten half-pains.
- **Filter for repetition.** State up front you're looking for things they do *repeatedly*. One-time work isn't skill-shaped.
- **Get three pieces per task: what, how long, how.** The "how" — the actual workflow — is what a skill encodes; without it you have a wish list, not a skill candidate.

A clean version of the ask:

> Pull up your calendar from the last two weeks. What were the 3 tasks that ate your time most — things you do repeatedly? For each: what were you doing, how long did it take, and how were you doing it (the workflow)?

**For L3/L4 users, swap the framing.** Advanced users repeat skills, not prompts. Ask: *"Which 2–3 skills do you call most often, and do you ever call them in the same sequence back-to-back?"* A repeated skill sequence usually points at a meta/orchestrator skill.

**No-calendar users:** swap "calendar from the last two weeks" for *"walk me through what yesterday actually looked like, hour by hour."* Same three pieces per task.

## Synthesizing into activities

After the calendar + the interview, you should be able to name 5-8 recurring activities with rough time estimates. Examples:

- Morning email triage — 45 min/day
- Weekly client status updates — 2 hrs/week
- Pulling notes from past calls before new ones — 30 min, 3-4x/week
- Friday roll-up of the week's wins — 1 hr/week
- "Looking up where that doc went" — 15 min, 3x/day

Time estimates are rough on purpose. Ask the user to ballpark. Precision here is fake precision.

This list feeds Phase 3 (scoring). If you skipped synthesis-first in Phase 2 and went straight to interview, do it now before scoring.
