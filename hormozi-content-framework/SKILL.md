# Hormozi Content Framework (Mozi Minute)

This is the simple 4-part structure Alex Hormozi uses to write good content when stuck on what to make. Use it whenever Shaw wants a piece of content built around a single insight backed by lived experience and persuasive reasons.

## The Framework

Every piece has exactly four parts, in this order:

1. **One main idea / lesson** — A single, sharp, declarative claim. Not a topic, not a question — a *point of view*. If you can't say it in one sentence, it's not ready.
2. **A story (or 2-3 small ones)** — Concrete examples that *demonstrate* the idea in action. Real people, real numbers, real outcomes. Stories do the persuasive heavy lifting because they make the abstract tangible.
3. **As many reasons as you can list (NOT steps)** — This is the most-missed part. Reasons explain *why* the idea is true from multiple angles. They are not a how-to. The goal is volume and variety: psychological, economic, behavioral, competitive, etc. Aim for 4-6+ distinct reasons, each labeled (A, B, C…).
4. **Soft CTA** — A gentle, low-pressure suggestion to apply the idea. No "BUY NOW." Just "if you sell X, consider Y."

## Why this works (so you can apply it flexibly, not robotically)

- **One idea** keeps the reader's brain from splitting attention. Multi-idea posts feel "smart" but convert worse because there's nothing to remember.
- **Stories** earn the right to make the claim. They prove you've actually done the thing, not just theorized it. Specifics (dollar amounts, names, exact mechanics) matter way more than polish.
- **Reasons (not steps)** because the goal of this kind of content is *belief change*, not instruction. Steps tell people *how*; reasons make them *want to*. If someone is convinced, they'll figure out the how. If they're not, the steps are wasted.
- **Soft CTA** because hard CTAs in idea-content feel like a bait-and-switch. The content itself is the value; the CTA just points at the door.

## How to draft

When Shaw gives you a topic or raw thought:

1. **Find the one idea.** Compress whatever he gave you into a single declarative sentence. If he gave you three ideas, ask which one or pick the sharpest and set the others aside.
2. **Mine for stories.** Ask Shaw for a real example, or pull from context (recent calls, clients, his own business). 1 strong story or 2-3 short ones. Include specifics: numbers, names where appropriate, before/after.
3. **Brainstorm reasons.** Push for *more* than feels natural. Try to hit different angles: psychological, economic, behavioral, structural, competitive, perceptual. Label them A, B, C, D, E, F. Each reason should be 1-3 sentences.
4. **Write a soft CTA.** One sentence. "If you [situation], consider [action]." That's it.

## Format

Write the post as flowing prose with the four sections clearly identifiable but not necessarily labeled in the final output (Hormozi only labels them when teaching the framework). Default to *no* heavy formatting — no bullet headers, no bold everywhere. The reasons section is the one place where labeled letters (A, B, C...) are appropriate because it signals "here come multiple angles."

## Worked Example (the canonical one Hormozi uses)

**1. Idea/lesson:** Adding physical products to intangibles dramatically increases conversions and profits.

**2. Story 1:** A gym owner in Gym Launch wanted to go online. It started working but he wasn't making enough per sale — charging $2-3K like everyone else once they learned high-ticket VSL ads. He wanted a $10K price point, so he bundled in all the home equipment required for his program. Average ticket jumped from $2-3K to $10-15K. Margin on the equipment was higher than what he was getting paid for the service. He scaled.

**Story 2:** A Hormozi Hotline call with an ACQ Vantage member who taught women to print and sell stickers. She was running 5-day challenges into a $27/mo community but couldn't generate enough cash upfront to fund ads. Advice: bundle in the sticker printing machine + a year of community access for $500-$1000. Result: 15-30x in price with likely no meaningful drop in conversion, plus continuity.

**3. Reasons:**
A) When people don't know exactly what the underlying costs are, they can't comparison-shop, which lets you price premiumly.
B) All-in-one solutions sell better than part-of-a-solution.
C) Physical products cost significantly less than consumers think, so margin stays huge when bundled with intangibles.
D) People are habituated to buying *stuff*; they're far less used to buying intangibles, so they convert higher at higher prices when stuff is involved.
E) Physical products take less explanation — eyes do the work. You can buy a car in five minutes off a lot. Try selling a $50K intangible that fast.
F) Including a physical product signals real expenses and investment, which makes the business harder to copy.

**4. Soft CTA:** If you sell services or intangible products like information and media, consider adding a physical product to fetch a premium in pricing and a boost in conversions.

## Common failure modes to avoid

- **Smuggling in a second idea.** If you find yourself writing "and another thing…" — stop. That's a second post.
- **Writing reasons that are actually steps.** "First, do X. Then do Y." That's a how-to, not a reason. Reasons answer *why is this true*, not *how do you do it*.
- **Vague stories.** "I knew a guy who made more money" is not a story. Numbers, mechanics, and outcomes are non-negotiable.
- **Hard CTA.** "DM me to book a call" kills the trust the post just built. Keep it soft.
- **Too few reasons.** Three is the floor. Push for five or six. Volume of angles is part of what makes the format persuasive.

## When not to use

This skill is for belief-change, top-of-funnel content built around a single idea. If the goal is explicit conversion — selling a specific offer, driving signups by end of the piece — use conversion-copy instead. A Mozi Minute can pair with a P.S. link, but the frame should be idea-first, not pitch-first.
