# Call Follow-Ups

Patterns for following up after a research call, expert network sync, or discovery call. These emails confirm alignment, provide next steps, and keep momentum.

Read this file before drafting any post-call follow-up email.

---

## Structure

Shaw's call follow-ups tend to follow this shape, though the specifics vary by follow-up type:

1. **Warm opener** — "Really enjoyed our call yesterday!" (or similar). Always genuine, never filler.
2. **Reaction** — One sentence showing Shaw was present. This takes different forms depending on context: for talk/event follow-ups where someone pitched an idea, use "Loved your [concise 3-4 word description]." For post-training or post-workshop follow-ups, a genuine reaction to how the session went works better (e.g., "I'm glad we were able to make it through a full loop of the framework"). For proposal follow-ups, skip this entirely and go straight to the value.
3. **Value / recap** — Provide something useful: a talk outline, a resource, a summary of what was agreed, or suggestions for next steps. This is the meat of the email.
4. **Closing** — Match the closing to whether you need a response. "What do you think?" when Shaw needs a reply to move forward (confirming a talk direction, reviewing a proposal). An open door ("Let me know if any questions or blockers come up :)") when the next meeting is already booked and nothing is blocking.

**Sign-off:** "Cheers, Shaw" (these are warm, peer-level relationships post-call).

## Template: Workshop/Talk Follow-Up

**When to use:** After a call where Shaw and the person agreed on a talk topic for an event series. The goal is to confirm the direction and share a narrative structure.

**Subject line:** "Call follow-up"

```
Hey [First Name],

Really enjoyed our call yesterday!

Loved your [3-4 word description of their talk idea]. I think this would be super valuable to the target audience for this series.

Here's a general talk outline that works well:
- About you
- Status quo (i.e. how [their specific domain] is done now)
- Problem (i.e. why the status quo sucks)
- Solution (i.e. how AI helps)
- Demo or details on solution
- Q&A

What do you think?

Cheers,
Shaw
```

**Personalization rules for the compliment line:**
- Keep the idea description to 3-4 words. Concise beats precise.
- React to their IDEA, not their credentials.
- Good: "Loved your agent skills for analytics talk idea"
- Good: "Loved your metric creation use case"
- Good: "Loved your talk idea on how AI is changing data platforms"
- Bad: "Loved your impressive background in data engineering and your thoughts on metric creation" (too long, evaluative)

**Talk outline customization:**
- The status quo parenthetical should reference their specific domain (e.g., "how business metrics are built now", "how analytics tasks are done now", "how data platforms are managed now")
- Keep it generic enough that it works as a starting framework, not a finished plan
- The outline is a bulleted list — this is one of the allowed exceptions to the "no bullet points" rule

## Template: Proposal Follow-Up

**When to use:** After a sales call where the next step is sending a proposal or outline PDF. The proposal has the details — the email just hits the key terms (what, how, how much) and points to the attachment.

**Subject line:** "Call Follow-up"

```
Hey [First Name],

I enjoyed our chat [yesterday/on Thursday/etc.]!

Here's what I'm thinking for the [training/engagement] geared directly toward your [their specific team/function].

- *[Format: e.g. Two-part Session: Primer + Done-With-You Workshop]* ([total hours] total)
- Virtual live session via *[platform]*
- *Total investment*: $[amount]

More details are provided in the attached PDF.

What do you think?

Cheers,
Shaw
```

**Key principle:** Keep the email body short. The proposal PDF carries the detail — don't re-explain workshop content, use cases, or session breakdowns in the email. The bullets are just enough for the recipient to know the shape of the deal (format, delivery, price) before opening the attachment.

**Optional P.S. line:** If the recipient mentioned needing supporting docs for internal approval (e.g., a CV for L&D submission), add a `P.S.` noting the extra attachment.

## Template: General Call Follow-Up

**When to use:** After a discovery call, sales call, or research call where the next step is NOT a talk outline.

**Subject line:** "Call follow-up"

```
Hey [First Name],

Really enjoyed our call [yesterday/on Thursday/etc.]!

[COMPLIMENT — one sentence reacting to something specific from the call]

[VALUE — recap of agreed next step, resource, or info you promised to send]

[CLOSING QUESTION — one question to move things forward]

Cheers,
Shaw
```

## Template: Post-Workshop/Post-Training Follow-Up

**When to use:** After Shaw has delivered a 1:1 workshop, training session, or intensive. The session already happened — the goal is to recap what was covered, suggest what to build next, and point to the upcoming check-in.

**Subject line:** "Call follow-up"

```
Hey [First Name],

Really enjoyed our session yesterday!

[SESSION REACTION — one sentence reacting to a shared accomplishment from the session, e.g. "I'm glad we were able to make it through a full loop of the 4-step framework."]

Here are some suggestions on what to build:

- [~3 items drawn from the session notes — see examples below]

Looking forward to our check-in [next week / on Wednesday / etc.]. Let me know if any questions or blockers come up in the meantime :)

Cheers,
Shaw
```

**Key principles:**
- Frame next steps as *suggestions*, not a committed plan. Peer tone, not PM tone.
- Cap at ~3 items. Even if the session notes list more, pick the highest-leverage ones.
- No "Loved your X" compliment — react to the shared experience instead.
- No "What do you think?" — the check-in is already on the calendar, so use an open door instead of forcing a reply.

**Examples of what suggestion bullets might look like:**
- "Keep improving the [tool] skill" (continue something started in session)
- "Build a similar skill for [adjacent tool]" (extend to adjacent use case)
- "Build a [tool] skill so you don't need to manually [tedious task] anymore!" (tackle a pain point, framed with the relief it brings)

## Threading Rules

- **Default:** Create as a reply in the existing email thread using `threadId`.
- **Exception:** If the last message in the thread is a Gmail emoji reaction (e.g., a heart or thumbs up), create the follow-up as a **fresh new thread** with no `threadId`. Gmail treats emoji reactions as messages, and replying to them causes rendering issues.
- **Subject line:** Always "Call follow-up" for post-call emails, whether threaded or fresh.

---

## Batch Follow-Ups

When following up with multiple people after calls (e.g., after a day of back-to-back research calls):

- **Use a consistent template.** Don't force variation across recipients. The same structure with swapped personalization is better than 5 different email styles.
- **Only customize:** the greeting name, the compliment line, and the status quo parenthetical in the outline.
- **Present the template to Shaw first** before creating drafts, so he can approve the structure and tweak wording. Then batch-create all drafts at once.
