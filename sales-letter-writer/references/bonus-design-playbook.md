# Bonus Design Playbook

How to design bonuses that actually move the needle, based on Hormozi's $100M Offers principles. A "good bonus" isn't random extra stuff. It's a lever on the value equation that makes saying yes feel stupid to refuse.

## The 7 principles of good bonuses

### 1. Every good bonus moves the value equation

A bonus is only "good" if it clearly does at least one of these, ideally two or more:

- ↑ Increases the dream outcome
- ↑ Increases perceived likelihood of success
- ↓ Decreases time delay
- ↓ Decreases effort & sacrifice

If you can't point to which variable it moves, it's fluff. Cut it.

### 2. It's directly tied to the core outcome (not random swag)

The bonus should solve a specific concern or obstacle about why they "can't" get the result, OR solve the next problem they'll logically hit after they buy.

- **Core offer = main problem solved**
- **Bonuses = "Yeah, but what about ___?" pre-solved**

If the bonus doesn't make the core promise more believable, faster, or easier, it doesn't belong.

### 3. High perceived value, low additional cost

The best bonuses are:
- Things you already do or have (templates, recordings, docs, SOPs) so marginal cost is near zero
- Tools and checklists, not just "more training," because they lower effort and time

You're trying to expand the price-to-value gap by *adding value*, not by cutting price.

### 4. Broken out and stacked, not bundled and vague

Same work, different packaging. One big vague "program" feels like 1 unit of value. The same program split into clearly named pieces and presented as separate bonuses feels like many units of value.

That's why infomercials keep going "but wait, there's more" — each named, priced bonus widens the perceived gap between what they pay and what they get.

### 5. Each bonus is sold with its own mini-argument

For each bonus you should be able to:

1. Give it a specific name
2. State what it is and what it does
3. Explain how it makes things faster / easier / safer
4. Paint the picture of life after they've used it
5. Justify a believable standalone price (no fake $10,000 PDFs — credibility kills if the price feels invented)

### 6. The bonus stack should feel bigger than the core offer

Psychologically, buyers think: *"If these are just the bonuses, the main thing must be insane."*

- Aim for bonus stack value that eclipses the core in perceived worth.
- Use scarcity/urgency on specific bonuses ("only if you enroll by X") to put the whole thing on steroids.

### 7. Pre-flight checklist

Before adding a bonus, ask:

1. Which value-equation variable does this move?
2. Which specific objection or "next problem" does it kill?
3. Does it cost me almost nothing extra to deliver?
4. Can I explain it in 2–3 sentences and justify a real price?
5. Does it make the stack feel obviously "too good to pass up"?

If it doesn't clear that bar, it's not a good bonus. It's noise. Strip it, or upgrade it until it actually moves the needle.

## How to brainstorm bonuses for a Shaw offer

Step 1: List the **objections and "next problems"** the buyer will have:
- "What if I can't keep this going after the engagement ends?"
- "What if my team can't pick it up?"
- "What if everything changes in 3 months?"
- "What if I screw it up / it breaks something?"
- "Where do I even start?"
- "What do I do on Day 31?"

Step 2: For each objection, ask: *what asset or session do I already have (or could create cheaply) that would kill it?*

Step 3: Audit each candidate against the 7 principles. If it doesn't move a value-equation lever and doesn't kill a real objection, drop it.

Step 4: Pick 2–5 bonuses. Fewer high-quality bonuses beats many weak ones. Each one should have a name, a one-sentence what-it-is, a one-sentence what-it-does-for-you, and (in your head, if not in the copy) an honest standalone price.

## Common Shaw bonus archetypes

These tend to work well for Shaw's Claude/AI offers:

- **Starter pack of pre-built artifacts** (skills, prompts, templates) — kills "where do I start," moves ↓ time delay and ↓ effort
- **Team handoff session + SOP** — kills "how do I scale this beyond me," moves ↑ dream outcome and ↑ likelihood
- **Follow-up check-ins (6-week, 12-week)** — kills "what if everything changes in 3 months," moves ↑ likelihood and ↓ risk
- **Personal access channel (Slack/Loom)** — kills "what if I get stuck," moves ↑ likelihood and ↓ effort
- **Roadmap document or roadmap session** — kills "what do I do after," moves ↑ dream outcome

Avoid:
- Generic "PDF guides" with no specific use case
- "Bonus training videos" that just add more work for the buyer
- Bonuses that overlap with the core deliverables (it cheapens both)
- Bonuses you don't actually have or can't deliver consistently

## Pushing back on weak bonuses

When Shaw proposes a bonus that doesn't pass the checklist, say so honestly. A weak bonus doesn't just fail to add value — it actively *cheapens* the rest of the stack by signaling you're padding. Better to have 2 strong bonuses than 5 mixed ones.

Example pushback: *"This doesn't move a value-equation lever and it doesn't kill a specific objection — it's a deliverable, not a bonus. I'd either upgrade it to attack a real objection (e.g., the '6-week/12-week check-in' angle that kills 'what if things change') or cut it."*
